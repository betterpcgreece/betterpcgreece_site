Form Factor

Motherboard Formats

The motherboard determines which components fit in your build. Bigger boards offer more expansion slots; smaller ones save space.

Full Size

ATX

The standard for most desktop builds. Plenty of PCIe slots, RAM slots, and room to grow.

Size: 305 × 244 mm

Mid Size

Micro-ATX

A balanced choice. Slightly smaller than ATX, still supports most CPUs and expansion cards at a lower price.

Size: 244 × 244 mm

Compact

Mini-ITX

Tiny footprint for small form factor cases. Limited to one PCIe slot and two RAM sticks — but impressively capable.

Size: 170 × 170 mm

Enthusiast

E-ATX

Extended ATX for workstations and HEDT platforms. Maximum expansion, dual CPU support on some boards.

Size: 305 × 330 mm

Memory

RAM Generations

System memory speed and capacity affect multitasking, creative workloads, and gaming frame rates.

Current Gen

DDR5

The modern standard for modern platforms. Higher bandwidth and on-die ECC. Kits regularly baseline at 6000+ MT/s for optimal processing sweet-spots.

Voltage: 1.1 V · Max speed: 8400+ MT/s

Previous Gen

DDR4

Still widely used. Affordable, broadly compatible with legacy and budget configurations. Sweet spot: 16–32 GB at 3200–3600 MHz.

Voltage: 1.2 V · Max speed: ~5333 MT/s

Legacy

DDR3

Found in legacy setups. Fine for basic office use, but a bottleneck for modern heavy processing requirements.

Voltage: 1.5 V · Max speed: ~2133 MT/s

Very Old

DDR2

Severely limited bandwidth and old standard architecture — suitable only for classic legacy hardware preservation.

Voltage: 1.8 V · Max speed: ~1066 MT/s

Storage

Storage Types

Where your OS lives defines how fast your PC feels. For most builds, an NVMe SSD for your OS drive is the right call.

Recommended

NVMe SSD

PCIe-connected M.2 drives. Modern generations reach exceptional speeds—effectively instantaneous for OS and app loops.

Speed: 3,500–7,000 MB/s

Budget

SATA SSD

2.5" or M.2 form factor, capped by the legacy SATA interface. Massive upgrade over any mechanical spinning drive.

Speed: ~550 MB/s

Mass Storage

HDD

Mechanical spinning disk. Unbeatable cost-per-GB for large files, arrays and backups — just avoid deploying your core OS here.

Speed: 80–160 MB/s

Processor

What to Know About CPUs

The processor is the brain of your build. Choosing one comes down to your workload: gaming favors clock speed, creative work favors core count.

Core Count

Cores & Threads

More cores handle more tasks seamlessly. Video rendering, engineering simulations, and heavy stream setups benefit most.

Performance

Clock Speed

Measured in GHz. High clocks maximize single-threaded tasks. Short peak operational frequencies are handled by built-in boost steps.

Generation

Architecture

IPC increases mean newer architectures at identical clock rates will outpace legacy iterations reliably.

Thermals

TDP & Cooling

Thermal Design Power guides your choice of thermal solutions. High power outputs require efficient, robust cooling blocks.

Graphics

Graphics Cards

The GPU is the most important component for gaming and visual workloads. It renders every frame you see on screen.

VRAM

Memory Matters

VRAM holds processing textures. Modern apps and resolutions scale aggressively, making baseline capacities highly important.

1080p Rec: 8 GB · 4K Rec: 16 GB+

Upscaling

DLSS / FSR / XeSS

Intelligent scaling engines allow graphics hardware to compute configurations at lower dimensions and display higher dynamic targets.

Standard: AI Generation Frame Integration

Ray Tracing

Lighting & Shadows

Simulates structural physical paths of light rays. Beautifully demanding computations requiring hardware-accelerated cores.

Power Draw

PSU Planning

Heavy graphics accelerators pull massive operational loads. Budget matching line capacities with overhead margins safely.

Infrastructure

Power & Cooling

The foundation of a stable system. Quality clean delivery and proper heat dissipation ensure long hardware life cycles.

Efficiency

PSU Ratings

80 Plus certifications (Bronze, Gold, Platinum) represent clean transformation efficiency. High certification translates to less waste heat generation.

Modularity

Cable Layouts

Fully modular units support unpluggable custom configurations, maximizing internal airflow spacing inside modern chassis structures.

Air Cooling

Tower Heatsinks

Highly reliable mechanical structural setups utilizing metal fin systems and continuous fan operations for simple long term execution.

Liquid Cooling

AIO Liquid Systems

Liquid loops routing heat to large radiator matrices. Perfect for managing aggressive computing frequencies and burst spikes smoothly.
