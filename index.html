Form Factor

Motherboard Formats

The motherboard determines which components fit in your build. Bigger boards offer more expansion slots; smaller ones save space.

Full Size

ATX

The standard for most desktop builds. Plenty of PCIe slots, RAM slots, and room to grow.

Size: 305 × 244 mm

Mid Size

Micro-ATX

A balanced choice. Slightly smaller than ATX, still supports most CPUs and expansion cards at a lower price.

Size: 244 × 244 mm

Compact

Mini-ITX

Tiny footprint for small form factor cases. Limited to one PCIe slot and two RAM sticks — but impressively capable.

Size: 170 × 170 mm

Enthusiast

E-ATX

Extended ATX for workstations and HEDT platforms. Maximum expansion, dual CPU support on some boards.

Size: 305 × 330 mm

Memory

RAM Generations

System memory speed and capacity affect multitasking, creative workloads, and gaming frame rates.

Current Gen

DDR5

The modern standard for Intel 12th gen+ and AMD Ryzen 7000+. Higher bandwidth and on-die ECC. Kits start at 4800 MT/s and scale to 7200+ for enthusiasts.

Voltage: 1.1 V  ·  Max speed: 8400+ MT/s

Previous Gen

DDR4

Still the most widely used standard. Affordable, broadly compatible with Intel 12th/13th gen and AMD Ryzen 5000. Sweet spot: 16–32 GB at 3200–3600 MHz.

Voltage: 1.2 V  ·  Max speed: ~5333 MT/s

Legacy

DDR3

Found in systems from roughly 2010–2017, pairing with Intel 2nd–4th gen and early AMD FX/AM3+ platforms. Fine for office use and light gaming, but a major bottleneck for anything modern.

Voltage: 1.5 V  ·  Max speed: ~2133 MT/s

Very Old

DDR2

Common from roughly 2004–2010 alongside early Core 2 Duo/Quad and AMD Athlon/Phenom chips. Severely limited bandwidth and capacity — only suitable for basic, low-demand systems today.

Voltage: 1.8 V  ·  Max speed: ~1066 MT/s

Storage

Storage Types

Where your OS lives defines how fast your PC feels. For most builds, an NVMe SSD for your OS drive is the right call.

Recommended

NVMe SSD

PCIe-connected M.2 drives. Gen 4 reaches 7 GB/s sequential reads — effectively instantaneous for OS and game loads.

Speed: 3,500–7,000 MB/s

Budget

SATA SSD

2.5" or M.2 form factor, capped by the SATA interface. Still a massive upgrade over any spinning drive.

Speed: ~550 MB/s

Mass Storage

HDD

Mechanical spinning disk. Unbeatable cost-per-GB for large media libraries and backups — just don't put your OS here.

Speed: 80–160 MB/s

Processor

What to Know About CPUs

The processor is the brain of your build. Choosing one comes down to your workload: gaming favors clock speed, creative work favors core count.

Core Count

Cores & Threads

More cores handle more simultaneous tasks. Video editing, 3D rendering, and streaming benefit most. Gaming rarely needs more than 8 cores.

Performance

Clock Speed

Measured in GHz. Higher clocks mean faster single-threaded tasks, which is most relevant for gaming. Boost clocks are short bursts above base.

Generation

Architecture

Each new generation improves instructions-per-clock (IPC). A newer lower-clocked CPU often beats an older higher-clocked one.

Thermals

TDP & Cooling

Thermal Design Power tells you how much heat a CPU produces. Higher TDP chips need beefier coolers — don't skip this step.

Graphics

Graphics Cards

The GPU is the most important component for gaming and visual workloads. It renders every frame you see on screen.

VRAM

Memory Matters

VRAM holds textures and scene data. Modern AAA games at high resolutions can demand 10–12 GB. 8 GB is the current minimum for 1080p, 12–16 GB for 1440p and 4K.

Recommended 1080p: 8 GB  ·  4K: 16 GB+

Upscaling

DLSS / FSR / XeSS

AI-driven upscaling lets a GPU render at lower resolution and output at higher quality. DLSS (Nvidia), FSR (AMD), and XeSS (Intel) can dramatically improve frame rates.

Best-in-class: DLSS 3.5 (Frame Gen)

Ray Tracing

Lighting & Shadows

Hardware ray tracing simulates real light physics. Demanding but increasingly standard in modern titles. Nvidia RTX cards lead here; AMD has caught up on recent generations.

Power Draw

PSU Planning

High-end GPUs pull 300–450 W alone. Always budget headroom in your power supply — add CPU TDP, then add 20% for safety margin.
